Guided Learning Workflow
Age Target: 10 Years Old

Turn a hard topic into a child-sized learning loop.

Best default: adult in the loop, AI as translator and coach, source-grounded mode when facts matter, and no final answer until the child has tried to explain or solve something.

Input Contract

1. Child profile

Age or grade, current skill level, and what usually trips them up.

2. Interests

Sports, animals, music, Minecraft, baking, comics, dance, or whatever keeps attention high.

3. Exact problem

Use the real worksheet, screenshot, paragraph, or homework prompt, not a vague summary.

4. Success target

Define what understanding means: explain it back, solve one similar problem, or spot a mistake.

5. Session mode

Choose hints only or full explanation before the AI starts talking.

1 Map prerequisites before simplifying

Ask AI to identify the main idea, missing building blocks, and likely misconceptions for a 4th-5th grader.

2 Force a fixed explanation structure

Use the same order every time: one-sentence explanation, three-step version, analogy, worked example, wrong answer, checks, similar problem.

3 Add one concrete analogy and one visual

Tie the concept to the child's interests, then switch representation with a mind map, timeline, or simple sketch plan.

4 Use a worked example and a wrong example

Show the reasoning out loud, then surface one tempting mistake and explain exactly why it breaks.

6 Recheck after 24 to 48 hours

Use one short retrieval question later. If the child cannot recall it, the issue is usually the learning loop, not motivation.

Delayed recall Retention test Keep it short

Guardrails

Guided tutor, not answer machine

Ask for hints first and make the model ask one question before moving to the next step.

Source-grounded when facts matter

Use NotebookLM or a source-limited prompt when the topic needs trustworthy factual accuracy.

Adult in the loop

Best default for a 10-year-old is adult review, especially before letting the child use a tool directly.

Watch access and privacy rules

Avoid personal data in prompts, and do not assume every learning tool is designed for direct under-13 use.

Strongest test: the child can explain the idea in their own words, solve one similar problem, spot one wrong answer, and still remember the idea tomorrow.
